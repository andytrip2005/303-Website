The Spread of Misinformation on Social Media

By Moosa Malik, Andy Tripathy, and Iba Abbasi

Why This Matters

Misinformation is no longer just a side-effect of social media. It's part of its core algorithmic structure. The platforms we rely on for news are also spreading false or biased content at scale, often without user awareness. This issue has social, political, and psychological implications. With deepfake technology and AI-generated content, the problem is escalating.

Methodology

  • Participants: 84 university students aged 18–23
  • Survey Tool: Google Forms (15 quantitative & qualitative questions)
  • Collected Metrics: Platform usage, perceived credibility, confidence in detection, peer trust, and reaction types
  • Tools Used: Cleaned and Processed in Microsoft Excel

Respondent Locations

Platform Usage

The most frequently used platforms among students were TikTok and Threads, followed by YouTube and Facebook. Each platform is designed for high-speed content delivery, often without rigorous fact-checking.

Most Misleading Platforms

According to students, TikTok and Facebook were identified as the worst platforms for misinformation. These platforms are heavily algorithmic and prioritize engagement over accuracy.

Confidence in Detecting Misinformation

The majority of students are not confident in identifying misinformation. Only 6% considered themselves "very confident," showing a major media literacy gap.

Reactions to Misinformation

Most students either Google the information or ignore it. A smaller number report or attempt to correct it, while only 8.2% said they share it.

Trust in Peers

Only 21% of students said they trust their peers to detect misinformation. 33% were unsure, and 46% did not trust their peers at all.

Recommendations

  • Embed Media Literacy Education: Universities should include media literacy courses or workshops as part of general education requirements to equip students with tools to spot and combat misinformation.
  • Platform Accountability: Social media companies should invest in AI tools that detect false information, with clear labelling and less reach for unverified content.
  • Student Engagement: Encourage peer-led initiatives where students can fact-check trending topics collaboratively and raise awareness through clubs or campus events.
  • Faculty Involvement: Professors should integrate digital discernment discussions into class discussions like humanities, social sciences, and communication courses.
